It is July 21, 2026. This is your Daily Dump of tech news.

Anthropic has reached a one-point-five billion dollar settlement in a landmark copyright lawsuit. The agreement concludes litigation brought by a coalition of major publishers over the unauthorized use of tens of thousands of books in the training set for the Claude series of models. While this closes one specific legal chapter, the broader industry debate remains unresolved regarding the use of copyrighted material for training large language models. The case specifically addressed whether the ingestion of entire copyrighted works for generative AI output constitutes fair use under current intellectual property law.

In the music industry, Sony Music Entertainment is suing the AI music generator Udio. The lawsuit alleges copyright infringement regarding thirty thousand songs, including tracks from artists ranging from Elvis Presley to Beyonce and Harry Styles. Sony asserts that the platform ingested its proprietary sound recordings to train deep-learning models capable of mimicking the vocal styles and instrumental arrangements of its signed artists. The plaintiff seeks statutory damages of up to one hundred fifty thousand dollars per infringed work, citing the unauthorized creation of derivative works that compete directly with the original artists in the marketplace.

Overseas, European regulators have issued a six-hundred-twenty-five-million-dollar fine to AliExpress. This is the largest fine yet under the European Union's Digital Services Act, following the platform's failure to implement mandatory safety and content fixes. Regulators cited the presence of prohibited products and illicit content that remained accessible despite multiple formal notices issued over the last eighteen months. The penalty represents approximately three percent of the company’s total global annual turnover, adhering to the enforcement guidelines established to ensure compliance with the bloc’s digital safety standards.

On the regulatory front, the Federal Communications Commission is preparing to retroactively ban devices from specific manufacturers. The agency intends to use its authority to target hardware companies suspected of operating as front entities for DJI, potentially removing their previously approved equipment from the United States market. This action targets hardware that utilizes the same underlying flight controller firmware and communication protocols as banned DJI models but is marketed under secondary brand names. Current federal guidelines require the revocation of equipment authorization for products identified as posing an unacceptable risk to national security.

In security, researchers have identified a widespread vulnerability affecting millions of vehicles across the United States. Dealership-installed alarm systems, often left in cars even when not requested by the buyer, contain flaws that allow attackers to remotely unlock, track, and disable the vehicles. The vulnerability exists in the cellular-to-CAN bus bridge, which lacks sufficient encryption for incoming remote commands. These aftermarket units, which often rely on legacy 3G or unstable 4G LTE connections, grant unauthorized users the ability to gain persistent access to vehicle diagnostic ports and secondary control modules.

The United States Army has instructed personnel to limit their use of generative AI tools. Internal communications revealed that service members were rapidly depleting allocated AI tokens, prompting a directive to restrict usage to mission-critical tasks. The consumption rate of tokens had exceeded the fiscal projections set for the current quarter, leading to a temporary suspension of non-essential accounts. The directive mandates that all remaining compute resources be routed through secure, air-gapped instances of enterprise-grade language models to ensure compliance with data classification protocols for sensitive operational information.

Finally, the Space Force is expanding its procurement requirements. The administration has requested a budget increase to support up to thirty billion dollars in rocket launch contracts, a move designed to accommodate the significantly higher volume of upcoming national security space missions. This funding is earmarked for the procurement of heavy-lift launch services over the next five years, replacing older contracts that provided for a significantly lower cadence of orbit insertions. The expansion reflects a shift toward a decentralized satellite architecture, requiring a greater number of individual launches to replace larger, monolithic legacy spacecraft currently reaching the end of their operational lifespans.
