It's July 3. This is your Daily Dump of tech news.

Google, with the FBI and international law enforcement, has announced a major blow against NetNut, one of the largest residential proxy networks. NetNut covertly rented access to two million compromised home devices, turning them into relays for malicious traffic. Google's Threat Intelligence Group announced they have substantially reduced NetNut's usable devices. This isn't just a disruption; it's a significant degradation of an operation linked to cybercrime, including credential stuffing and data exfiltration. This coordinated takedown highlights a growing focus on disrupting cybercrime infrastructure, making it harder for criminals to operate.

In Apple news, the company is reportedly gearing up for a significant push into the foldable device market, with plans to manufacture around ten million units of a new foldable iPhone Ultra. Industry sources indicate these premium devices are expected to retail for approximately two thousand five hundred dollars. This strategic move is Apple's effort to capture market share in an emerging, high-margin segment, as the company also plans an upgraded iPad Pro line and a redesigned entry-level MacBook Pro for next year. These releases coincide with the iPhone's twentieth anniversary, suggesting a broader refresh. The ambitious production target for the foldable iPhone Ultra suggests Apple's confidence, despite mixed success from other foldable manufacturers.

Over in gaming hardware, both Sony and Xbox are accelerating their shift away from physical media. Sony’s PlayStation disc factory is reportedly being repurposed. The company confirmed all new PlayStation games released after January two thousand twenty-eight will be sold exclusively digitally. Meanwhile, Xbox is reportedly considering dropping disc drives from future consoles. More intriguingly, they are testing a feature allowing players to digitize physical game collections into digital licenses. This move, if implemented, would mirror PlayStation’s trajectory and signal the end of disc-based console gaming for new releases from both major players within a few years.

Turning to AI, Google’s agentic assistant, Gemini Spark, is now officially available on Mac. This assistant, designed to operate as a proactive, twenty-four-seven digital agent, has received improvements, including enhanced real-time tracking and support for more applications. The Mac release significantly expands Google's agentic AI efforts, allowing more developers and users to integrate Spark's capabilities into daily workflows. This means complex, multi-step tasks can now be automated directly from desktop environments, streamlining everything from data analysis to project management.

In security news, iOS twenty-seven is set to introduce a new framework to help apps detect real-time social engineering scams. This feature aims to identify and flag potential fraud as it unfolds, analyzing communication across voice calls, text messages, and emails. By providing apps with tools to analyze communication patterns and content in real time, Apple is establishing a new, proactive layer of defense against sophisticated phishing and impersonation attacks, which often exploit human trust. This move represents a significant step towards protecting users from scams difficult to counter with traditional technical safeguards.

Meanwhile, a deeply concerning incident highlights the persistent threat of state-sponsored surveillance: a European politician, actively investigating spyware abuses, had their phone hacked with Pegasus spyware. A government customer of NSO Group reportedly used Pegasus to target this politician. The irony is profound, as the politician served on an EU committee tasked with examining the very spyware industry that enabled this attack. This incident underscores the pervasive nature of state-sponsored surveillance and its reach, even against those working to mitigate its dangers. It raises serious questions about accountability and ethical boundaries in the global spyware market.

Finally, a candid assessment from Mark Zuckerberg: he has reportedly informed Meta staff that the company’s AI agents haven’t progressed as quickly as he’d hoped. During an internal meeting, the Meta CEO acknowledged that AI development was not moving at the anticipated pace. This frank admission offers a rare glimpse into the significant challenges of scaling advanced AI technologies. It suggests that even with substantial investment and resources, the path to fully realized, highly capable AI remains complex and potentially much slower than both public expectations and ambitious internal timelines.
